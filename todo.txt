Note: the original RHS picture had "k·c_{1,k}" varying per row; correct
form (block-mult of Claim 1) is constant c_{1,m-1} multiplied by k:
          | 1·c_{1,m-1}
U(m-1)c1' | 2·c_{1,m-1}
          | 3·c_{1,m-1}
          ...

   Remark (future usage, no code yet):

   The block identity is stated using c_1 only because the residue 1
   generates Z/mZ additively. Any other generator works just as well
   with the same U(m). For m prime, every a ∈ {1,…,m-1} generates
   Z/mZ, so we get m-1 parallel identities — one per row c_a of C_red
   — all sharing the same U(m) / U(m-1) infrastructure.

   Caveat: L(m) is "below the diagonal" in the natural integer order,
   which is not symmetric under the (Z/mZ)* action. The literal
   identity for c_a is

       (L(m)+U(m)) · Π_a · c_a = block-form with Π_a-conjugated U(m-1)

   where Π_a is the permutation i ↦ a·i mod m. In code Π_a is just
   an index relabelling; no real matrix multiply needed.

   Why it matters for waldicone (potential, gated by profiling):

   - For m prime, when an enumeration sweep changes any single q_k,
     pick a ≡ -k^{-1} mod m; under σ_a the changed coordinate becomes
     "last", and the c_a recursion gives an O(m) constraint update
     instead of O(m²) (vs. the c_1-only version, which only saved
     work when q_{m-1} was the variable). So "incremental update on
     any coordinate" — not just the last.

   - For m composite, only a ∈ (Z/mZ)* (φ(m) residues) give clean
     recursions; the rest don't generate the full chain.

   - The m-1 identities are not independent — they all follow from
     the Kunz relations — but together they may give a cheaper
     feasibility certificate for cone membership than the standard
     O(m²) inequality scan. Open question.

   Next step (only if profiling shows our enumeration over Normaliz
   output is the bottleneck): derive Π_a-conjugated block form for
   each c_a, build a small Π_a table, prototype an incremental
   constraint check on a prime-m sweep, benchmark vs. the straight
   recompute. Until then: keep this remark, don't refactor.

72) [DONE] full picture of the map S(g, l=1, m, μ) → S(g+1, l=2, m, μ, missing)

Both sets are indexed by subsets M ⊆ {1, …, μ−1} where
M = {r : w_r = m+r}. The lift map L (M ↦ M, with the only change
being w_μ jumping from 2m+μ to 3m+μ — equivalently, 2m+μ
becomes a gap) lands in the level-2 missing pile iff M is
sum-avoiding for μ:
  ∀ a, b ∈ M (allowing a = b),  a + b ≠ μ
equivalently, M ∩ (μ − M) = ∅ and μ/2 ∉ M when μ is even.
Otherwise 2m+μ becomes reachable as (m+a) + (m+b) and the
"lifted" candidate collapses back to level 1.

Genus shift, uniform across strata:
  g_{l=2, missing} = g_{l=1} + 1.

Stratification by k = |M| = m + μ − 1 − g_{l=1}:

   k                level-1 stratum   level-2 missing stratum   killed Δ(k)

ANALYSIS — what the count actually looks like.

Encode a level-2 semigroup with multiplicity m and Frobenius
residue μ by the Apéry exponents k_r ∈ {0, …, l} where
w_r = (k_r + 1)·m + r and f = w_μ − m. At level 2:
  r ∈ {1,…,μ−1}:    k_r ∈ {0, 1, 2}        ("left", 3 choices)
  r = μ:            k_μ = 2                forced
  r ∈ {μ+1,…,m−1}:  k_r ∈ {0, 1}           ("right", 2 choices)

Three counters classify a level-2 semigroup:
  M = {r ∈ {1,…,μ−1} : k_r = 0},   k = |M|
  p = #{r ∈ {1,…,μ−1}\M : k_r = 2}      (left flips)
  q = #{r ∈ {μ+1,…,m−1} : k_r = 1}      (right flips)

GENUS IDENTITY (clean):
  g = m + μ − k + p + q.

KUNZ AT LEVEL 2 — only one constraint binds.
The general Kunz inequality is
  no carry (a+b < m):   k_{a+b}     ≤ k_a + k_b + 1
  carry  (a+b ≥ m):     k_{a+b−m}   ≤ k_a + k_b + 2
With k_r ≤ 2 the carry case is automatic. The no-carry case
binds only when k_a = k_b = 0, giving k_{a+b} ≤ 1. Two
consequences:
  (a) ∀ a, b ∈ M with a+b = μ: forces k_μ ≤ 1, contradicts
      k_μ = 2. ⇒ M must be sum-avoiding for μ.
  (b) For r = a+b ∈ {1,…,μ−1}\M with r ≠ μ: k_r is forced to
      1 (cannot be 2).
Define M̃(M) := (M+M) ∩ ({1,…,μ−1}\M) \ {μ}.
Then |M̃| left positions lose their k_r = 2 option.

WHY THE FACTORIZATION FAILS — and exactly where.
The naive ChoicesLeft = C(μ−1−k, p) ignores M̃. The correct
count is C(μ−1−k−|M̃(M)|, p), and |M̃(M)| depends on the
*specific* M, not just |M|. So the count cannot factor as
Strata(k) · Flips(p, q).

FIRST FAILURE CASE (g = 7).
At m = 4, μ = 3: M = {1} ⇒ 1+1 = 2 ∈ {1,2}\M, so |M̃| = 1
and p = 1 is forbidden. M = {2} ⇒ 2+2 = 4 (carries to 0),
|M̃| = 0, p = 1 fine. The naive formula counts p = 1 for
both, overcounting by exactly 1 — the +1 diff at g = 7. The
growing diffs at g = 8..14 are all M̃-misses summed across
(m, μ).

THE THREE-INDEX COUNT (correct closed expression).
Refine the sum-avoiding count to a bigrade
  S(μ, k, j) := #{ M ⊆ {1,…,μ−1} sum-avoiding for μ
                   with |M| = k and |M̃(M)| = j }.
Then
  N(g, l=2, m, μ) = Σ_{k,j,p,q : k−p−q = m+μ−g}
                    S(μ, k, j) · C(μ−1−k−j, p) · C(m−1−μ, q).
Item 72 verified the j = p = q = 0 slice: S(μ, k, 0) is the
"missing" pile, equal to C(⌊(μ−1)/2⌋, k) · 2^k. The total
above is just convolution against the right binomial
C(m−1−μ, q) and the left binomial C(μ−1−k−j, p).

WHAT WOULD BE NEEDED.
To get an explicit N(g, l=2, m, μ): iterate over all 2^(μ−1)
subsets of {1,…,μ−1}, keep the sum-avoiding ones, tabulate by
(|M|, |M̃|) to obtain the table S(μ, k, j), then convolve
with C(m−1−μ, q) for each m and with C(μ−1−k−j, p).
Computationally fine (O(μ · 2^μ) per μ); good for verifying
the truth sequence {0,1,1,3,7,12,23,44,79,143,254,447,785}
for g = 2..14.

OUTLOOK.
A clean closed form for S(μ, k, j) likely does not exist:
|M̃(M)| is a restricted sumset count, an additive-combinatorics
problem with no obvious nice generating function beyond the
j = 0 orbit formula. We don't even have a clean lower bound
for the total N(g, l=2). Good for now — leave the bigrade
enumeration as a future implementation if/when needed.
